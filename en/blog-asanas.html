Hatha Yoga

From Instinct to Enlightenment — Asanas as the Key to Inner Evolution

3 January 2026 · Reading time: 10 min

Walk into any yoga class in the world and you will see people moving through postures: stretching, balancing, twisting, inverting. You might assume you understand what is happening — a form of physical exercise with mindfulness added. But classical yoga suggests something far more interesting is taking place, if the practice is understood correctly.

Asanas, in the classical tradition, are not exercise. They are not even primarily about the body. They are a system — refined over thousands of years — for working with the energy body, stimulating the chakras, purifying the channels through which prana flows, and systematically preparing the entire human instrument for the experience of its own deepest nature.

What Is Asana, Really?

The word asana comes from Sanskrit — from the root as, meaning "to sit" or "to be." Patanjali's famous definition in the Yoga Sutras is elegantly simple: sthira sukham asanam — "that which is steady and comfortable is asana."

Notice what is absent from this definition: any mention of difficulty, flexibility, strength, or physical challenge. The classical understanding of asana is first and foremost a state — a quality of stability, ease, and comfort — not a shape or position. The shape is simply the vehicle through which that state is cultivated.

"Sthira sukham asanam" — Steady and comfortable is asana.
— Patanjali, Yoga Sutras 2.46

Originally, asana referred almost exclusively to meditative sitting positions. Patanjali described just a few. The extensive system of hundreds of asanas we have today was developed primarily within the Hatha Yoga tradition, which emerged and expanded between roughly the 9th and 15th centuries CE — as a systematic methodology for transforming the body into a vehicle capable of sustaining very high levels of pranic energy and prolonged meditative states.

Asana vs. Exercise: A Fundamental Difference

One of the most important — and most commonly misunderstood — distinctions in yoga is the difference between asana and physical exercise. They look superficially similar but are physiologically opposite in their effects:

Yoga AsanaPhysical Exercise
Breathing slows downBreathing speeds up
Metabolism decreasesMetabolism increases
Oxygen consumption decreasesOxygen consumption increases
Body temperature dropsBody temperature rises
Reduces catabolism (tissue breakdown)Stimulates catabolism
Activates parasympathetic nervous systemActivates sympathetic nervous system
Mind becomes inward and stillMind becomes outward and active

This is not to say one is better than the other. Both have value and appropriate contexts. But conflating them leads to a serious misunderstanding of what yoga practice is actually doing. When a yoga class is taught as a workout — emphasising challenge, effort, physical achievement, and calorie burning — it is teaching exercise, not yoga, regardless of the Sanskrit names applied to the positions.

Asanas and the Energy System

If the primary purpose of asana is not physical fitness, what is it? In classical yoga, the answer is clear: asanas work primarily with the pranamaya kosha — the energy body — rather than the physical body. The physical body is the secondary beneficiary.

The human energy body consists of:

  • Prana — the vital life force that animates all living matter
  • Nadis — the energy channels through which prana flows (72,000 in total, with three main channels: Ida, Pingala, Sushumna)
  • Chakras — the energy centres or vortices located at key points along the Sushumna (central channel), from the base of the spine to the crown of the head
  • Kundalini — the fundamental evolutionary energy lying dormant at the base of the spine, which when awakened moves upward through the chakras producing progressive expansions of consciousness

Classical asana practice systematically works with all of these. Different postures compress, stretch, and stimulate different parts of the body — and correspondingly stimulate the chakras and nadis in those regions. Some asanas specifically activate and awaken Kundalini energy; others regulate the flow of prana in the nadis; others purify specific chakras.

The Animal Kingdom Connection

Why do so many yoga postures bear the names of animals? This is one of the most fascinating aspects of the classical asana tradition, and its answer reveals the depth of the system.

The ancient rishis (sages) and yogis observed the animal kingdom with extraordinary attention. They noticed that animals maintained their health, vitality, and balance naturally — without medicine, without intervention — through the characteristic postures and movements that were natural to them. The cobra naturally stretches its spine upward. The cat naturally arches and curves its back. The fish opens its chest and throat to the sky.

These natural postures were not accidents. Each represented a particular relationship with gravity, a particular pattern of energy distribution, a particular way of being in the body. By imitating these postures deliberately and consciously, the yogis discovered they could access the corresponding energetic states and qualities.

But there is a deeper significance. Many classical texts describe the asana system as a map of evolution — a sequential progression from the most primitive forms of life (serpents, fish, insects) through the animal kingdom (cow, eagle, peacock, lion) to the upright human being and ultimately to the cosmic forms (mountain pose, tree pose, cosmic dancer). Practising the full sequence of asanas is like rehearsing the entire journey of consciousness from its most contracted expression to its most expanded.

The Role of Awareness

What distinguishes yoga asana from mere body position is awareness. The classical texts are emphatic: an asana performed without awareness is not yoga — it is gymnastics. The quality of awareness brought to the practice is what determines its effect on the energy body and mind.

In practice, this means that while holding an asana, the attention is directed not primarily at the external shape of the body, but at the internal experience: the flow of breath, the sensation of prana in different regions, the quality of mental activity, the state of the chakras. The body becomes a laboratory for inner observation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is asana in yoga?
Asana in yoga means a state of stability, peace, and comfort. According to Patanjali's Yoga Sutras, asana is a position that is both comfortable and stable. Originally, asana was developed as a preparation of the body for sustained meditation — not as a physical exercise system.
What is the difference between asanas and physical exercises?
When performing asanas, breathing and metabolism slow down, oxygen consumption decreases, and body temperature drops. Physical exercise has the opposite effect. Yoga postures reduce catabolism while exercises stimulate it. Both have value — but they serve entirely different purposes.
How do asanas work with the energy system?
Practising asanas stimulates the chakras and distributes prana (Kundalini energy) throughout the body. About 35 classical asanas are specifically designed to awaken and distribute Kundalini energy, while the remaining asanas regulate and purify the nadis, facilitating the smooth flow of prana.
Why are yoga asanas named after animals?
Many asanas are inspired by animals and represent a progression through evolutionary stages — from the simplest forms of life to the most complex: the fully conscious human being. The ancient yogis observed nature carefully and discovered how to maintain health, balance energies, and develop consciousness by imitating the characteristic postures of various animals.
