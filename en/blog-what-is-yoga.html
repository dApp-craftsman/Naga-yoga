Yoga Philosophy

What Actually Is Yoga?

1 November 2025 · Reading time: 6 min

If you asked ten people what yoga is, you would likely hear ten different answers. A fitness class. A form of stretching. A stress relief technique. Meditation. A spiritual practice. A lifestyle. Each answer holds a fragment of truth — yet none captures the whole picture.

The word yoga comes from the Sanskrit root yuj — to unite, to connect, to join. But what is being united? In the classical tradition, yoga is the science of uniting the individual consciousness with the universal. It is not a physical discipline with spiritual benefits — it is a complete system for the total development of the human being.

A Science, Not a Religion

Classical yoga predates all organised religion. The oldest systematic text on yoga — Patanjali's Yoga Sutras — was compiled approximately 2,000 years ago, though the oral tradition it encodes is far older. Yet even this ancient text is not a religious scripture. It is a manual — a systematic description of the mind, consciousness, and the methods by which a human being can transcend ordinary perception.

Yoga makes no demands on what you believe. It asks only what you are willing to experience. This is what makes it a science: its results can be verified in direct experience, and its methods work regardless of your cultural background, faith, or philosophical outlook.

"Yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ" — Yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind.
— Patanjali, Yoga Sutras 1.2

More Than Postures

In the West, yoga has become almost synonymous with physical postures — asanas. This is a misunderstanding that the classical tradition would find puzzling. In Patanjali's eightfold path (Ashtanga Yoga), asana is just one of eight limbs. The other seven include:

  • Yama — ethical principles: non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing, moderation, non-possessiveness
  • Niyama — personal observances: cleanliness, contentment, discipline, self-study, surrender
  • Pranayama — expansion and regulation of vital energy through breath
  • Pratyahara — withdrawal of the senses from external objects
  • Dharana — focused concentration
  • Dhyana — sustained meditation
  • Samadhi — the state of absorption in pure consciousness

Together, these eight limbs form a complete path of development — from the outermost layer of human behaviour all the way to the innermost layer of pure awareness.

The Layers of the Human Being

Classical yoga understands the human being not as a body that happens to have a mind, but as a multi-layered system. The Taittiriya Upanishad describes five koshas — sheaths or layers of existence:

  • Annamaya kosha — the physical body, sustained by food
  • Pranamaya kosha — the energy body, sustained by prana (life force)
  • Manomaya kosha — the mental body, the realm of thoughts and emotions
  • Vijnanamaya kosha — the intellect body, the faculty of discrimination and wisdom
  • Anandamaya kosha — the bliss body, the causal layer closest to pure consciousness

Each layer of yoga practice addresses a different kosha. Asanas work primarily with the physical and energy bodies. Pranayama works with the energy and mental bodies. Meditation penetrates to the deeper layers of intellect and bliss.

Why Classical Yoga Matters Today

Modern life has given us extraordinary capabilities — but also extraordinary fragmentation. We are more connected than ever yet feel more isolated. We have more information than any generation before us, yet experience more confusion. We can manage almost everything in the external world, yet we remain largely helpless before our own mental states.

This is precisely the problem classical yoga was designed to address. Not the lack of flexibility. Not the need for better posture. But the deeper question of what it means to be human — and whether a human being can become the master of their own mind and life.

In Summary

  • Yoga is a complete science for total human development
  • It works on all five layers of the human being — physical, energetic, mental, intellectual, and causal
  • Postures (asanas) are just one of eight components in the classical system
  • The goal of classical yoga is not flexibility or fitness — it is the direct experience of your own deepest nature
  • Yoga is non-dogmatic: its methods work regardless of religion, culture, or background

If you have felt that yoga classes left something important out — if you have sensed there is a deeper tradition beneath the surface — you are right. Classical yoga is not a watered-down spiritual fitness programme. It is a sophisticated system that has guided human beings toward genuine transformation for millennia.

And it is available to you — in its full depth — exactly as it was transmitted.
