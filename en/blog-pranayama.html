Pranayama

Pranayama — The Bridge to the Unknown

10 December 2025 · Reading time: 10 min

Every spiritual tradition on Earth has recognised that breathing is not merely a mechanical process. Ancient Indians called the life force prana. Chinese medicine calls it qi. Japanese medicine calls it ki. Ancient Greeks called it pneuma. The Hebrew tradition calls it ruach. Different names — the same fundamental insight: there is a force that animates living beings, and the breath is its primary vehicle.

Pranayama — the yoga of prana — is the science of working directly with this force. The word comes from Sanskrit: prana (life force) + ayama (expansion, extension, regulation). Pranayama is not simply breathing exercises. It is the systematic expansion and direction of the vital energy that underlies all of our physical, mental, and spiritual functioning.

Prana: More Than Oxygen

It is important to understand from the outset: prana is not simply oxygen or air. Prana permeates everything — it is the animating principle of all living matter. Oxygen is its physical carrier, just as radio waves are the physical carrier for music. The wave is not the music; oxygen is not prana.

According to classical yoga, prana in the human body has five major manifestations, called the pancha pranas:

  • Prana — the life force governing the upward movement in the chest region; related to breathing, heartbeat, and sensory intake
  • Apana — the downward-moving force governing elimination and reproduction
  • Samana — the balancing force at the navel centre governing digestion and assimilation
  • Udana — the upward-moving force in the throat governing speech, growth, and the process of death
  • Vyana — the all-pervading force that distributes energy throughout the entire body via the nadis

Pranayama works directly with these pranic forces — amplifying, balancing, and redirecting them.

The Breath-Mind Connection

Of all the yogic insights, perhaps none is more immediately verifiable than this: the state of the breath mirrors the state of the mind — and vice versa. When you are anxious, the breath becomes shallow and rapid. When you are angry, the breath becomes powerful and forceful. When you are in deep concentration, the breath slows almost to a halt.

This is not coincidence. The breath and the mind are governed by the same underlying pranic force. The Hatha Yoga Pradipika states directly: "When prana moves, chitta (mind) moves. When prana is still, chitta is still." This bidirectional relationship is the key to pranayama's power.

We cannot directly command the mind to be still — anyone who has tried knows this. But we can command the breath. And by consciously directing the breath, we indirectly bring the mind to stillness. This is why pranayama is described as the bridge between the physical body and the mind — and why Patanjali lists it as the fourth limb of his eightfold path, immediately before the practices of sensory withdrawal and meditation.

The Main Classical Pranayama Practices

The classical texts describe numerous pranayama techniques, each with specific effects. The most foundational include:

  • Nadi Shodhana (alternate nostril breathing) — purifies the nadis, balances the hemispheres of the brain, creates mental clarity and calmness
  • Bhastrika (bellows breath) — energises the body, clears the lungs, generates heat and awakens prana
  • Kapalabhati (frontal brain cleansing) — purifies the respiratory system, tones the abdominal organs, energises the nervous system
  • Ujjayi (victorious breath) — creates internal heat, stimulates the vagus nerve, promotes deep concentration
  • Bhramari (humming bee breath) — calms the nervous system, alleviates mental tension, prepares for meditation
  • Surya Bhedana (solar piercing breath) — activates solar energy, increases metabolism and vitality, warms the body

How to Practise Nadi Shodhana (Alternate Nostril Breathing)

1
Posture: Sit comfortably with the spine erect — on a chair or cross-legged on the floor. Close the eyes.
2
Hand position: Bring the right hand to Nasika mudra — index and middle fingers rest on the forehead between the eyebrows; thumb controls the right nostril, ring and little fingers control the left.
3
Inhale: Close the right nostril with the thumb. Inhale slowly and completely through the left nostril. Count to 4.
4
Hold: Close both nostrils. Retain the breath comfortably for 1–2 seconds without strain.
5
Exhale: Release the right nostril. Exhale slowly and completely through the right nostril. Count to 4.
6
Complete the round: Inhale right, hold, exhale left. This is one complete round. Start with 5 rounds; gradually build to 15–20.

The Role of Kumbhaka (Breath Retention)

One of the most distinctive features of classical pranayama is kumbhaka — deliberate breath retention. There are two types: antara kumbhaka (retention after inhalation, lungs full) and bahya kumbhaka (retention after exhalation, lungs empty).

Classical texts place enormous emphasis on kumbhaka. The Hatha Yoga Pradipika states: "Pranayama is perfected through kumbhaka." During breath retention, something remarkable happens: the mind, deprived of the sensory input that breathing normally provides, naturally turns inward. Distracting thoughts reduce. The gap between thoughts widens. This is the closest the beginning practitioner comes to the experience of meditation.

"When prana is still, the mind is still. When the mind is still, the yogi attains liberation — of this there is no doubt."
— Hatha Yoga Pradipika 4.29

Pranayama and Modern Science

Modern research has begun to validate what classical yoga described thousands of years ago. Studies have shown that pranayama practices produce measurable changes in:

  • Autonomic nervous system balance (increased parasympathetic tone)
  • Heart rate variability (a key marker of resilience and adaptability)
  • Cortisol and stress hormone levels
  • Brainwave patterns (increased alpha and theta activity)
  • Blood pressure and cardiovascular function
  • Immune system markers

These are not marginal effects. Well-designed studies show significant improvements comparable to — and in some cases exceeding — pharmaceutical interventions, without any of the side effects.

Yet for all that science can measure, it cannot yet capture what the classical yogis were pointing at: the experience of prana as a living, intelligent force — not merely a physiological parameter. The bridge of pranayama leads not just to better health, but to the direct experience of the life force itself. And from there, as Patanjali described, to the nature of consciousness.
